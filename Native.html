Gray Catbird

The Gray Catbird is a member of the family Mimidae, and can be commonly mistaken for the Dark-Eyed Junco, another gray bird found in the city. However, the Gray Catbird is distinctly larger. The Catbird is solid gray with in both its body and wings, with a distinct black cap, usually the key signifier in its identification, except for a russet patch under its long tail. While it may be difficult to spot it along the dirt and branches, they pop out against the bright foliage and leaves. Catbirds reside in New York City all year, and are not very shy around people, though are more reserved than starlings and sparrows. They are easily found wooded areas of Central Park, with many sightings in the Halett Nature Sanctuary and the Ramble.

Hover To Hear

The Gray Catbird was named for the sound of its call, which is distinctly shrill and sounds eerily alike to a cat's meow. Like the other members of the Mimidae family, Catbirds are capable of mimicking the calls of other birds, and even other animals.

Blue Jay

The iconic Blue Jay is widely known for its bright blue feathers and distinct, tufty head. Belonging to the family Corvidae alongside the Common Raven and American Crow, it stands out from its close relatives due to its vivid display. The Blue Jay can be found all throughout the city, as these bold birds are less afraid of humans than most, and are even less afraid of other birds and animals. Its territorial behavior and ability to mimic other predators gives the Blue Jay an edge against other birds.

Hover To Hear

Blue Jays are known for their very distinct and shrill call, almost always immediately accompanied by another jay. These birds never back down from a challenge over food or territory.

Northern Cardinal

The Northern Cardinal is one, if not, the most iconic bird in the East Coast. It's easy to take this bright red bird for granted when you travel elsewhere and can't find any local birds that can live up to the same standards. It might even be hard to believe that such an iconic bird actually exists! The Northern Cardinal belongs to the Cardinalidae family, related to Tanagers, Grosbeaks, and the unfortunately named Dickcissel. Male Northern Cardinals are bright red with a black mask and orange beak, while female Cardinals are more muted: brown with tinges of red around its tufted head, wing, and tail tips.

Hover To Hear

Northern Cardinals are known for their sharp chirps that sound more puckered than sparrows. Cardinals are known for being very territorial, so it's not unusual to hear entourages of such chirps when walking around the woods and forest floor.

American Robin

The "all-American" Robin was actually named by European colonizers due to its similar appearance to the European Robin. Though, the American Robin is larger and is classified as a completely different type of bird, the thrush, while its European "counterpart" is a flycatcher. Belonging to the family Turdidae, an unfortunate name if you ask me, the American Robin is related to other thrushes, such as the Hermit or Wood Thrush. The American Robin is one of the most famous birds in the North American continent due to its unique appearance and population boom in the spring. Seen as a spring bird in pop culture due to this behvaior, the American Robin is usually welcomed on lawns to pluck the early worm, and its distinct red chest and black feathers are always a delight to see.

Hover To Hear

American Robin songs sound like an ongoing escalator, just when you think the note is high enough, the robin finds a way to go even higher. This birdsong is a staple on the East Coast and in the city, you might've heard it without even knowing it come from the robin!
